  • Julian Schroeder Laboratory Intern (August 2019 - January 2020)
    In this project, we aimed to select, grow, and screen mutant candidates of Arabidopsis thaliana that exhibit the "hot" phenotype. We began by autoclaving the soil and sterilizing the seeds to ensure a controlled environment. Seeds were initially sown in a petri dish, and once mature seedlings developed, they were carefully transplanted into pots for further growth. To assess the phenotypic variations, we analyzed and compared the stomata and pavement cells of both stagnant and mutant plants using infrared cameras. This approach allowed us to gather detailed data on the physiological differences between the two groups, enhancing our understanding of the traits associated with the "hot" phenotype in Arabidopsis thaliana.
  • Eduardo Fricovsky Laboratory Intern (June-August 2018)
    The process of glycolysis provides a continuous energy source for cancer cells, fueling their growth and proliferation. The aim of this study was to identify an inhibitor that could effectively disrupt glycolysis, thereby stifling the growth of cancer cells by targeting the key regulatory enzyme, phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1). Sulforaphane, a naturally occurring compound found in cruciferous vegetables like broccoli, cabbage, and kale, was investigated for its potential as an inhibitor. As an organic isothiocyanate, sulforaphane has been documented to inhibit PFK-1 activity, which is crucial for energy production in cancer cells. Our results demonstrated that 0.15 mM sulforaphane effectively inhibited PFK-1 activity by 50%, providing promising evidence for its role in potentially controlling glycolysis and, consequently, cancer cell growth.
